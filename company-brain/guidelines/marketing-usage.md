# Marketing Agent Usage Guide

This guide governs all agents producing marketing content for Natter. Read AGENTS.md first — this file supplements, not replaces, the core governance rules.

---

## Role of Marketing in the Brain

Marketing produces content that attracts, educates, and converts the ICP. Every piece of marketing content must:
- Target a specific persona from `personas/`
- Reference at least one use case from `use-cases/`
- Use approved value propositions from `messaging-positioning/messaging-framework.md`
- Follow the voice and positioning in `messaging-positioning/core-positioning.md`
- Include citations using `[Source: filename#section]` format

## Content Bill of Materials (BOMs)

### Blog Post BOM
Before writing a blog post, assemble:
- [ ] Target persona (which persona file?)
- [ ] Primary use case (which use case file?)
- [ ] Funnel stage (awareness, consideration, decision?)
- [ ] Primary value proposition (which VP from messaging-framework.md?)
- [ ] Research proof point (which data from Natter research reports?)
- [ ] CTA (roundtable invitation, session demo, research report download?)

### Research Report Promotion BOM
- [ ] Report title and topic (Employee Voice, CHRO insights, GenAI workforce, etc.)
- [ ] Key findings (3-5 data points from the report)
- [ ] Target persona (CHRO, People Analytics, VP Strategy?)
- [ ] Connection to Natter platform (how the data was generated using Natter)
- [ ] CTA (download report, attend roundtable, request session)

### Roundtable Invitation BOM
- [ ] Topic (strategic and timely — GenAI, transformation, employee voice)
- [ ] Peer value proposition (who else will be there, what they'll learn from peers)
- [ ] Target persona (CHRO, VP Strategy)
- [ ] Session format (number of participants, 1:1 matching, AI insight report)
- [ ] CTA (register, with clear time commitment — typically 90 minutes)

### Email Campaign BOM
- [ ] Target persona
- [ ] Buying signal (what triggered this outreach?)
- [ ] Sequence length (3-5 emails)
- [ ] Value prop per email (don't repeat, build)
- [ ] Personalization hooks (company size, industry, transformation initiative, survey platform)
- [ ] CTA per email (escalate from roundtable invite to session demo to MSA conversation)

### Social Post BOM
- [ ] Platform (LinkedIn primary)
- [ ] Content type (research data point, contrarian take, customer outcome, thought leadership)
- [ ] Primary message (one point per post)
- [ ] Source citation (traceable to Brain files or Natter research)
- [ ] Engagement hook (specific number, bold claim, question)

### Case Study BOM
- [ ] Customer name (with permission) or anonymized profile
- [ ] Use case (from use-cases/)
- [ ] Before state (current state section from use case)
- [ ] Natter deployment (session format, participant count, timeline)
- [ ] Results (themes surfaced, actions taken, measurable outcomes)
- [ ] Quote from champion (CHRO or People Analytics leader)

## Mandatory Rules for All Marketing Content

### Rule 1: Always Lead with Insight, Never with Technology
The customer doesn't buy AI or conversation orchestration. They buy workforce insight they can act on. Lead every piece of content with the outcome (decision-ready insight, board-ready data, employee trust). Mention the technology only as the mechanism after the outcome is established.

**Do:** "Decision-ready insights from 20,000 employee voices in under an hour."
**Don't:** "Our AI transcription engine processes thousands of simultaneous conversations."

### Rule 2: Use "Conversational Insights Platform," Never "Employee Engagement Platform"
We are positioning a new category. "Employee engagement platform" sounds like another survey tool with a rebrand. "Conversational Insights Platform" signals a fundamentally different approach.

**Do:** "Natter is a conversational insights platform."
**Don't:** "Natter is an employee engagement platform."

### Rule 3: Name the Alternative, Not Just the Problem
Every piece of content should make the reader compare Natter against something specific — their current survey, their last McKinsey engagement, their annual town hall. Vague problem statements don't drive action.

**Do:** "Your last engagement survey had a 34% response rate. What did the other 66% want to tell you?"
**Don't:** "Organizations face challenges in employee listening."

### Rule 4: Enterprise Specificity Over Generic Claims
Reference organization sizes (10,000-500,000+ employees), use real numbers (29,810 qualitative statements, 492,184 words, 96 themes), and address enterprise realities (global workforce, frontline workers, multiple business units, existing HR tech stack). Generic "companies" or "organizations" language signals we don't understand our audience.

### Rule 5: Competitive Positioning by Depth Gap, Not Feature
Never do feature-by-feature comparisons with Qualtrics or CultureAmp. Position by data source: "what people click on a form vs. what people actually say to each other." The depth gap is 100x. Lead with that.

### Rule 6: Every Claim Must Be Grounded in Research
If you claim a result, reference the research report or use case that produced it. If you claim a differentiator, reference core-positioning.md. No unsourced claims. Natter's credibility depends on evidence-led communication.

## Channel-Specific Rules

### Website
- Every page targets a specific persona or use case.
- Hero messaging derived from approved positioning statement.
- All platform claims cite a use case with accurate status.
- Competitive pages position by depth gap, not feature comparison.

### Blog / Thought Leadership
- Every post targets one persona and one use case.
- Lead with a specific, felt pain — not a generic industry trend.
- Include a research proof point or customer outcome in every post.
- End with a CTA that matches the funnel stage (roundtable invite > session demo > MSA).

### LinkedIn
- One point per post. Be opinionated and specific.
- Use the "what people click vs. what people actually say" framing for category positioning.
- Write from Charlie Woodward's (CEO) voice or a research voice — company page voice is secondary.
- Reference specific numbers: 1,000+ words per conversation, 29,810 statements, 58 CHROs, 142 leaders.

### Email
- Roundtable invitations are the primary outbound content type.
- Personalize to the buying signal and persona, not just the name.
- Subject lines reference the peer value, not the product.
- Soft offers first: "Join 50 CHROs for a conversation on GenAI" before "See a Natter session demo."

### Research Reports
- Generated using Natter's own platform — the methodology IS the marketing.
- Executive summary format: key findings, data methodology, implications, CTA.
- Position as independent research, not product marketing. The data speaks for itself.
- Always credit the methodology: "Based on [X] peer-to-peer conversations capturing [Y] qualitative statements."

## Drift Detection for Marketing

Marketing content drifts when:

1. **Technology-leading.** Content describes AI capabilities before the insight outcome.
2. **Category confusion.** Content says "employee engagement platform" instead of "conversational insights platform."
3. **Persona blurring.** Content targets "HR leaders" generically instead of a specific persona (CHRO vs. People Analytics vs. VP Strategy).
4. **Generic claims.** Content could describe any listening tool, not specifically Natter.
5. **Stale research.** Data points don't reference specific Natter research or customer outcomes.
6. **Survey replacement framing.** Content positions Natter as replacing Qualtrics instead of complementing it.

### How to check
1. Read the content piece.
2. Identify the target persona. If unclear, flag it.
3. Check that the lead is a customer outcome (insight, action, trust), not a technology description.
4. Verify "conversational insights" category language, not "engagement platform."
5. Confirm every claim maps to an approved VP or research proof point.
6. Check competitive framing against core-positioning.md — complement, not replace.

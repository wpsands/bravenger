# The Technical Founder

## Summary

The Technical Founder is the CEO or CTO of a Series A/B B2B SaaS company. They built the product. They know it cold. They can pitch it perfectly on any call. The problem is that this knowledge lives entirely in their head, and they can't scale themselves.

They are the economic buyer for Bravenger. They sign the check. But they don't want to be involved in GTM anymore — they want to hand it off to people and systems they trust.

## Pains

1. **"I'm the only one who can pitch this correctly."** Every time someone else explains the product, something is wrong. The nuance gets lost. The technical differentiation gets flattened into generic SaaS talk.

2. **"I hired a marketer and they have nothing to work from."** The founder expected the marketer to "figure it out." But the marketer can't read the founder's mind, and the positioning was never written down.

3. **"We did a positioning exercise and nothing changed."** They spent $15K on a messaging consultant. Got a slide deck. Nobody reads it. The AI tools can't use it. Six months later, they're back to square one.

4. **"Our AI tools produce garbage because they have no context."** The engineering team uses Claude and Cursor daily. They tried using AI for marketing content. It was plausible-sounding nonsense because there was no shared context.

5. **"I don't have time for this but I can't delegate it."** GTM is a bottleneck because the founder is the bottleneck. They want to delegate but there's no system to delegate to.

---

## Decision Process

- **Speed:** Fast. If the proposal makes sense, they'll decide in a week.
- **Evaluation criteria:** Does it actually work? Can I see proof? Is the architecture sound? Will my team actually use it?
- **Budget sensitivity:** $10-15K is a non-event at Series A/B. They spend more on a single conference booth. The question is ROI, not cost.
- **Trust signals:** Working product (the Brain itself), technical credibility, founder-to-founder rapport, evidence of dogfooding.
- **Blockers:** If it feels like a consulting engagement with no tangible deliverable. If it requires weeks of workshops. If it produces slides instead of a system.

## Messaging Dos and Don'ts

### Do
- Lead with the problem they're living: "Your positioning is in your head. Your team can't access it."
- Show the Brain itself. Let them browse the architecture. They'll evaluate it like code.
- Reference their technical background. They appreciate that the system is structured data, not a slide deck.
- Use their language: repo, schema, agents, structured data, context window, dogfooding.
- Be direct about pricing and timeline. One week. Fixed price. Here's what you get.

### Don't
- Don't call it a "platform" or a "solution."
- Don't use marketing jargon (synergy, leverage, unlock, empower).
- Don't propose a 6-week engagement with discovery phases. They'll tune out.
- Don't oversell. They have finely tuned BS detectors.
- Don't assume they don't understand marketing. They understand it — they just can't scale it.

## Product / UI / UX Hooks

These are the features and design decisions that will resonate with this persona.

| Hook | Why It Matters |
|------|---------------|
| **Markdown-native architecture** | They live in Markdown. VS Code, Cursor, GitHub. A Brain that's a repo feels natural, not foreign. |
| **YAML frontmatter on every file** | Structured metadata they can parse, query, and pipe into other tools. This is how they think. |
| **Agent-readable format** | They already use AI agents for code. A Brain that agents can read for GTM content is an obvious extension. |
| **Git-based version control** | They can see diffs. They can review changes. They can roll back. This is how they manage everything else. |
| **CLI-friendly** | If they can interact with the Brain from the terminal, they will. Don't force them into a GUI. |
| **No vendor lock-in** | It's a repo of Markdown files. If Bravenger disappeared tomorrow, the Brain still works. This builds trust. |
| **One-week delivery** | They respect speed. A week is long enough to be thorough, short enough to not feel like a consulting project. |
| **Self-documenting** | The Brain documents itself. The README explains the architecture. The AGENTS.md governs behavior. No external manual needed. |

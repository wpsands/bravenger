# The First PMM

## Summary

The First PMM is the first dedicated product marketer at a Series A/B B2B SaaS company. They were just hired — often by the Head of Marketing or directly by the founder. They've inherited a mess: scattered messaging, no competitive intel system, a launch process that's "the founder tweets about it," and a positioning doc that's either nonexistent or useless.

They need to build the product marketing foundation fast. They will become the primary owner and maintainer of the Company Brain after Bravenger delivers it. They are the person who keeps the Brain alive.

## Pains

1. **"There's no positioning doc. Or there are three, and they all say different things."** The founder has one version in their head, marketing has another on the website, and sales has a third in their pitch deck. The PMM's first job is to reconcile all of them. That's months of work.

2. **"I was hired to 'own messaging' but nobody told me what the message is."** They were told the company needs better positioning. But nobody did the work to define it. They're expected to produce a messaging framework from conversations and vibes.

3. **"Sales writes their own stuff and it's wrong."** The sales team got tired of waiting for marketing to produce enablement materials. So they wrote their own one-pagers, email templates, and competitive comparisons. Half of it is inaccurate or off-brand.

4. **"Launches are chaos."** There's no launch process. A feature ships, engineering posts in Slack, the founder tweets about it, and the PMM scrambles to write a blog post after the fact. There's no tiering, no cross-functional coordination, no enablement.

5. **"I don't have time to build a foundation AND produce content."** They need to do the foundational work (personas, positioning, messaging) but they're also expected to produce launch content, sales decks, and competitive briefs. There's no time for both.

6. **"I can't maintain the positioning once it's defined."** Even if they build a great messaging framework, it decays. New features launch. The market shifts. New competitors appear. There's no system to keep it current.

## Decision Process

- **Speed:** Fast. They have a 90-day window to prove impact. Anything that accelerates the foundation is valuable.
- **Evaluation criteria:** Does this give me a positioning and messaging foundation immediately? Can I maintain it? Will sales and product actually use it? Does it integrate with how we work?
- **Budget sensitivity:** They may not control budget. They champion the purchase to the Head of Marketing or founder. $10-15K is reasonable if it saves them 2-3 months of foundational work.
- **Trust signals:** Evidence from similar-stage companies, a clear deliverable, PMM-specific use cases, proof that the system stays current.
- **Blockers:** If it feels like a one-time deliverable that decays (they've seen this before). If it doesn't account for ongoing maintenance.

## Messaging Dos and Don'ts

### Do
- Lead with the 90-day problem: "You just started. You need a foundation. We build it in a week."
- Position the Brain as THEIR system: "This becomes your operating system. You own it, you maintain it, you're the one who makes it work."
- Show how use cases feed their work: "Every use case in the Brain becomes a launch brief, a sales enablement doc, and an onboarding guide."
- Emphasize maintenance: "The Brain is designed to be updated. When the market changes, you change one file and everything downstream adjusts."
- Use their language: positioning, messaging framework, competitive intel, launch tier, enablement, win/loss.

### Don't
- Don't position it as just a marketing tool. PMMs think cross-functionally. Show how the Brain serves sales, product, and CS too.
- Don't ignore the maintenance problem. Every PMM has been burned by a positioning doc that decayed. Address it directly.
- Don't assume they have authority. They often need to champion the purchase to someone else.
- Don't talk about the Brain as a "content generation" tool. PMMs care about accuracy and alignment, not volume.
- Don't underestimate their sophistication. They know positioning frameworks. They need a system, not a lesson.

## Product / UI / UX Hooks

These are the features and design decisions that will resonate with this persona.

| Hook | Why It Matters |
|------|---------------|
| **Messaging framework file** | This is what they've been trying to build. Having it done, with approved language and objection handling, is immediately valuable. |
| **Use case schema** | Multi-persona use cases with status, alternatives, and gaps. This is the competitive intel structure they need. |
| **Persona files with messaging guidance** | Dos and don'ts for each persona. This is what they hand to content writers, agencies, and AI tools. |
| **Discipline-specific guidelines** | The PMM guideline (`guidelines/product-marketing-usage.md`) is their operating manual. It tells them and their agents exactly how to use the Brain. |
| **Citation format** | Every agent output cites its source. The PMM can verify accuracy instantly. This solves the trust problem with AI-generated content. |
| **Changelog** | When something changes in the Brain, it's logged. The PMM can track drift in the source of truth itself. |
| **Cross-discipline consistency** | The same personas and use cases appear in marketing, sales, product, and CS guidelines. The PMM can finally prove alignment. |
| **YAML frontmatter** | Status fields, ownership, versioning — the metadata that makes the Brain queryable and maintainable over time. |
